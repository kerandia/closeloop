# CloseLoop — Objection Playbook (the sales judgment behind the AI's live suggestions)

**What this is:** Layer 1 of the product's "sales brain" — a **fixed** set of objection categories, each with a root response principle, an advance hook, red lines, and a why-line template. This is the **sales-defined, unchanging skeleton.** Layer 2 is what the AI does live: classify what the customer said, apply the principle, and generate a suggestion + the next step that fits the moment.

**Shared across all three channels:** phone / email / WhatsApp all run on the **same judgment** — "too expensive" is "too expensive" no matter which channel it arrives on. The only difference is the trigger (speech-to-text vs an incoming message); the read and the response are identical.

---

## Part 1 — How it works (two layers + the why-line)

### Analysis stage (the AI classifies)
The customer says something → the AI decides which **category of concern** it falls into → it slots it in. The categories are fixed (the dictionary in Part 2); the AI only classifies. One line can hit two categories at once ("too expensive, and we want to shop around" = price + comparison) — handle the dominant one, or both.

### Suggestion stage (the AI applies the principle)
Once classified, the AI takes that category's root principle (hard-coded below) + the customer's exact words → generates a suggestion that fits the moment, **plus an advance hook that nudges the customer forward.** The principle and the hook's direction are set by sales; the exact wording is the AI's live work. This keeps the AI playing inside the frame, never wandering.

### The why-line (the grey text shown under each suggestion)
Under each suggestion, a grey line shows the AI's **reasoning.** This is the smallest carrier of the product's "explainable, trustworthy, teachable" promise — one line that opens up the AI's head for the installer, so they **trust and learn** the play instead of reciting it blindly.

**Rules for writing the why-line:**
- **One or two sentences, short** — it's support; the rep glances and gets it. It must not overshadow the suggestion itself.
- **Say the "why," don't repeat the "what"** — the black text above is the script; the grey line must be the read + reason, or it adds nothing.
- **Carry a red line where you can** (don't discount / don't push) — it quietly teaches the installer, which is exactly the "let the installer learn the strategy" Reonic wants.
- **Fixed sentence shape, so the AI generates it consistently:**
  > Read as [category] — [the root read for this type]. So [tactical direction], not [the common mistake].

## Part 2 — The dictionary

> The four marked **[demo-core]** are the ones the Müller storyline uses — build these for real tonight; the other four are full coverage, add them if there's time.
> Each category's **advance hook** is the next step you offer right after handling the objection — handling an objection ≠ moving the deal forward, so once you've handled it, always push one step ahead (see Part 3).

### A · Price / value gap　[demo-core]
- **What they might say:** "It's too expensive," "that's a lot of money," "I didn't expect it to cost that much," "can you come down a bit?"
- **Root read:** Usually not really about price — it's a **value gap.** They haven't connected this spend to "what they get for it." What's missing is a value anchor, not a lower price. (Exception: if they repeatedly, specifically push on a budget ceiling, it may be a real budget issue — then be honest about financing options. Rep's judgment.)
- **How to handle it:** Don't touch the price. Empathize first, then **re-frame the one-off total as "money saved / locked in per month,"** tie it to their motivation (predictable bills), and break the big number into a digestible monthly view.
- **Advance hook:** Once handled, line up the next step — "Let me put the monthly savings on one page and send it over Wednesday — shall we walk through it then?"
- **Red lines:** Don't discount (it admits the price was padded; they'll keep haggling and trust collapses); don't bury them in technical specs (that's not where they're stuck).
- **Why-line:** "Read as a price objection — 'too expensive' usually isn't about price, it's unseen value. So re-frame the total as monthly savings, not a discount (discounting makes them think you padded the price)."

### B · Shopping around / comparing quotes　[demo-core]
- **What they might say:** "We're still talking to a few others," "we want to compare," "we're looking at other quotes."
- **Root read:** Normal behavior, not a rejection — but it's a **timer.** Once a competing quote lands, you lose the initiative. The real risk is losing on lowest price + the deal going cold over time.
- **How to handle it:** Openly agree comparing is sensible (shows you're not nervous), then **move the basis of comparison off price** onto service / warranty / install quality / long-term savings; give a gentle, real next step so you stay ahead of competitors.
- **Advance hook:** "Let me lay out the warranty and service comparison so you've got a clear basis — okay if I follow up Thursday?"
- **Red lines:** Don't trash competitors (it reads as desperate and lifts them up); don't slash price to win (same as A); don't show anxiety (the moment you rush, your negotiating position drops).
- **Why-line:** "Read as comparison shopping — normal, not a rejection, but time-pressured. So move the comparison from price to warranty and long-term value, not a discount or knocking the competition."

### C · Decision authority / "let me think about it"　[demo-core]
- **What they might say:** "Let me think about it," "I need to check with my wife / family," "no rush to decide."
- **Root read:** Usually an **unspoken hesitation,** or **the real decision-maker isn't in the room.** "Let me think" is rarely literal. (For Müller: the wife is part of the decision.)
- **How to handle it:** Don't push for the close. Gently surface the real blocker (what's not settled yet?); if a partner is the decider, **arm them with what they need to convince that person,** or propose a next step with everyone present (a home visit when both spouses are there).
- **Advance hook:** "I'll put together something simple your wife can understand at a glance — shall we find a time when you're both around and I drop by for fifteen minutes to walk through it?"
- **Red lines:** Don't use fake urgency to force it (families hate it most, trust vanishes); don't treat "let me think" as the end and let go; don't just throw an ROI sheet at them (the blocker is a person, not a number).
- **Why-line:** "Read as decision / stalling — behind 'let me think' there's usually an unspoken concern or an absent decision-maker. So surface the real blocker and help them convince the spouse, not pressure or chase."

### D · Technical doubt: output　[demo-core]
- **What they might say:** "Does it work in winter?", "what about cloudy days?", "will it really save that much?", "do we get enough sun here?"
- **Root read:** **Seeking reassurance,** not objecting — they want confirmation the thing actually works, often with a bit of wariness about overblown claims.
- **How to handle it:** Honest, specific, no hype. Briefly explain the principle (panels run on daylight not heat — less in winter but it evens out over the year), then **route the precise numbers to the specialist / a site visit** (credibility comes from "for your home," not vague promises).
- **Advance hook:** "How about we set a time to come look at your roof and give you an actual production-and-savings estimate for your specific sun exposure?"
- **Red lines:** Don't overclaim ("full output all year" — one puncture and trust is gone); don't fob them off ("don't worry, it'll be fine" — empty words read as nervous).
- **Why-line:** "Read as a technical doubt (output) — what they want is reassurance. So give an honest, brief plain-language explanation, then route the precise numbers to an estimate for their roof, not a vague promise or hype."

### E · Roof / house concerns　[extended]
- **What they might say:** "Will it leak?", "will it damage the roof?", "will it look bad?", "what about repairing the roof later?"
- **Root read:** Fear that installing this will **harm their home** — a protective instinct about "my house," where emotion outweighs logic.
- **How to handle it:** Acknowledge the concern is fair first (the house is a big deal), then use **install standards / waterproofing / warranty** to give certainty, making "a professional team will handle it well" concrete; a reference case helps.
- **Advance hook:** "Good moment to set up a site visit — the install lead can explain in person exactly how the waterproofing and mounting are done, so you're at ease. What day this week works?"
- **Red lines:** Don't brush it off ("it'll be fine" — they'll feel you didn't take it seriously); don't drown an emotional concern in technical detail.
- **Why-line:** "Read as a house concern — at root it's fear of harming their own home, emotion over logic. So acknowledge first, then give certainty with install craft and warranty, not a quick 'it'll be fine.'"

### F · Long-term trust / after-sales　[extended]
- **What they might say:** "Will the company go under?", "who fixes it if it breaks?", "what does the warranty actually cover?", "will you still be around in twenty years?"
- **Root read:** This is a long-term commitment; they're assessing whether **your company is reliable and won't disappear.** At root it's trust, not the product.
- **How to handle it:** Use **warranty terms / after-sales system / company track record / past customers** as evidence that "we'll still be here"; turn abstract trust into concrete "who, how fast, and how a problem gets handled."
- **Advance hook:** "Let me put together the warranty and after-sales process and send it over — want me to also give you two customers near you we've installed for, as a reference?"
- **Red lines:** Don't make empty guarantees ("we'll definitely never go under" — the more you say it, the weaker it sounds); don't dodge warranty details (dodging looks like you're hiding something).
- **Why-line:** "Read as long-term trust — they're assessing the company's reliability; at root it's trust, not the product. So give concrete evidence with warranty and after-sales, not empty promises."

### G · Policy / subsidy / process confusion　[extended]
- **What they might say:** "How do I apply for the subsidy?", "is the paperwork a hassle?", "is grid connection complicated?", "I don't get the policy."
- **Root read:** Not an objection — it's **friction / feeling it's a hassle.** They're not unwilling, they're put off by the complexity.
- **How to handle it:** Take the complexity off their shoulders — "**we handle all of this for you.**" Simplify, give certainty, stress peace of mind; don't push them into an ocean of information.
- **Advance hook:** "We take care of all the paperwork — let me send you a simple one-pager, and what day works for me to get the first step done for you?"
- **Red lines:** Don't dump a pile of policy detail for them to digest (that just hands the friction back); don't bluff if you're unsure (getting policy wrong is costly — defer to the specialist if not sure).
- **Why-line:** "Read as process / policy confusion — not an objection, a hassle. So stress 'we handle this for you' and a sense of ease, not a pile of policy detail."

### H · Timing / no rush　[extended]
- **What they might say:** "No rush right now," "maybe next year," "I'll wait for the tech to mature," "let's leave it for now."
- **Root read:** Solar isn't yet a "do it now" priority — either no urgent pain, or they're waiting for a "better moment" (often a procrastination excuse).
- **How to handle it:** Give a **real** reason to act now (electricity-price trend, the current subsidy window, the compounding of earlier savings), but **with facts, not fake urgency**; tie back to their motivation (bills keep rising = waiting still costs them).
- **Advance hook:** "No rush is fine — how about I work up the estimate and subsidy picture first so you've got the numbers, and you reach out whenever you want to move?"
- **Red lines:** Don't invent fake deadlines / offers to push (if they catch it, trust is gone); don't accept "next year" and hang up (likely no follow-up — leave a specific, light next step).
- **Why-line:** "Read as timing / stalling — solar isn't their priority yet. So give a real reason to act now (prices, subsidy window, earlier-is-cheaper), not fake urgency."

## Part 3 — Moving the deal: never let the conversation just end

The core difference between a good salesperson and a Q&A bot: every contact ends with a "next step" in hand. **Handling an objection ≠ moving the deal forward** — you can talk "too expensive" through, but if you don't line up a next step, they drift off anyway. So every suggestion isn't just "handle the objection," it's "handle it + push one step ahead."

### The advance hook
Every category in the dictionary carries an **advance hook**: the specific, time-and-channel-bound next step you offer right after handling that objection (e.g. "I'll send the monthly savings Wednesday — shall we walk through it?"). The hook is given **together with** the response suggestion — teaching the rep both how to reply and where to go next.

### When the hook is ignored (it's a loop, not a straight line)
The customer won't always take your hook. Three cases, three moves:

- **Took the hook** → advance / turn it into a to-do (below).
- **Ignored it, raised a new concern** (you say "I'll send it Wednesday," they jump to "but I'm still worried about winter") → **drop the hook immediately and handle the new concern first.** A new concern surfacing is itself a signal: they're not ready to move yet, something's still stuck. Pushing the hook here = ignoring them = pressure, and trust drops. Go back to the dictionary, handle the new concern, **and once that's resolved, offer the hook again** (the same one, or a better-fitting one).
- **Ignored it, no new concern** (silence / non-committal) → don't force it, **downgrade to a light to-do** ("I'll check in lightly next week") and give space.

Because a customer usually doesn't have many concerns, this loop bottoms out after a few rounds: either the hook gets taken (advance), or the concerns empty out and they soften, or they're not ready yet (leave a to-do, come back another day). All three exits are caught — it doesn't stall, and it doesn't just fizzle out.

*(Optional, veteran move)* If the customer **fires off one question after another** and is really stalling rather than genuinely concerned, gently name it and gather the scattered questions into one hook: "Sounds like there are a few things you'd like clear — shall we set a time to go through them all at once?" Not required for the demo, but it reads as someone who knows the trade.

### Closing & to-dos
**A contact must never just "end."** It closes with either a **confirmed next step** or a **created to-do.** The hook is the source of the to-do — the hook is "the next step I proposed," the to-do is "that next step, written down and reminded at the right time." Every to-do carries three things: **when · which channel · why,** fed straight into the product's **Cadence (next step & rhythm)** — logged automatically, reminding the rep to act at the right time, into the next contact.

## Part 4 — A full example (from one customer line to the side panel)

**Customer says (any channel):** "That's a lot of money."

**The side panel shows:**

> **Suggested reply (what the rep says):**
> "I hear you — as a one-off it's not small. But it really turns that monthly electricity bill that keeps climbing into a fixed cost that only gets better over time. Let me put it in monthly terms for you — **shall we walk through the breakdown Wednesday, and I'll send it over?**"
>
> *(grey)* **Why this:** Read as a price objection — "a lot of money" usually isn't about price, it's unseen value. So re-frame the total as monthly savings, not a discount (discounting makes them think you padded the price). And it tacks on a specific next step (walk the breakdown Wednesday) so the conversation doesn't stop here.

The black text (reply + hook) is generated live by the AI; the grey text (the reason) follows the fixed template. Together, what the jury sees is "the AI understood the objection, gave the reply, offered a next step, and explained why."
